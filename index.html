Part 1a — Theory Overview

Mark Solms & The Biology of Attachment in Mammals

"Attachment is the primal addiction — the brain's reward system is fundamentally organised around proximity to attachment figures." — Solms, M. (2021). The Hidden Spring. Profile Books.

Mark Solms, building on the work of Jaak Panksepp, argues that the attachment behaviour is not a learned behaviour by mammals; instead, it is something that is hardwired into the brainstem. This instinct is present in all mammal species, irrespective of their degree of intelligence, and predates the evolution of the cerebral cortex altogether.

At the centre of Solms' theory is the PANIC/GRIEF circuit – the neurological component underlying attachment. When mammals are separated from their attachment figures, the levels of endorphins in their brains drops, and the mammals begin to feel panic. The return of the attachment figure and the reinstatement of those endorphin levels allows the mammals to feel calmed. Panksepp and Solms arrived at this concept through electrical stimulation studies across a variety of different mammalian species (Panksepp, 1998).

The complementary CARE circuit explains nurturing behaviour across all mammalian species. Whether it's a dog mothering tiger cubs or a human father sitting beside his hospitalised child at 4am, all of these behaviours are driven by the CARE circuit. Unlike fish and turtles, mammals require care after birth from the CARE circuit. Therefore, the CARE circuit is essential for the survival of mammalian species and their offspring.

Solms bridges the gap between Bowlby and neuroscience in this crucial insight: Bowlby's (1969) activation of the attachment system maps onto these ancient subcortical circuits. The attachment behavioural system is the PANIC/GRIEF circuit expressed in relational behaviour. According to Fonagy et al. (2014), attachment also directly impacts the brain's ability to regulate affect, attention, mentalisation, and self-agency – all crucial elements of personality development.

Part 1b — Personal Reflection

My Attachment Style: Secure (Type B)

"Security is not the absence of difficulty — it is having someone who shows up."

I identify with secure attachment as described by Van Rosmalen, Van IJzendoorn and Bakermans-Kranenburg (2014) which is the feeling of trust in the world and its predictability, which comes from good caregiving. While I did not grow up in a perfect household, I did grow up in a bicultural one.

My emotional world was informed by my Brazilian mother who was very emotionally expressive and my Swiss father who was more reserved in his expressions of love – two very different attachment styles from each other, yet both present in my upbringing. Although there can be various caregivers in an individual's upbringing, each relationship with those caregivers will be different based upon the quality of the relationship between the child and those caregivers (Van Rosmalen et al., 2014). Thus, my upbringing included these different attachment styles, yet my love from each of my parents provided for me created an attachment style for me that was successful regardless.

Formative Memory 1
Sailing with My Father
My father's presence on the water regulated my nervous system without saying a word. This is Bowlby's (1969) secure base in action – I felt secure through his presence.
Formative Memory 2
4am at the Hospital
When my mother rushed me to the hospital at 4am during a pericarditis episode, she instinctively reached for Solms' (2021) CARE circuit — our mammalian instinct to seek out nurturing and care from those around us whenever we are in need of it.

My father's later transformation in Hawaii illustrated to me the concept of the internal working model by Van Rosmalen et al. (2014). His shift in emotions indicates that he became more emotionally present towards me, his child.

Fonagy et al. (2014) confirm that individuals who are securely attached to someone are independently rated as having higher levels of resilience, self-reliance, a social orientation, empathy, and both the motivation and ability to form deeper relationships with others and higher levels of self-esteem. Furthermore, I experience conflict directly with the individual involved in the relationship, I am calm under stress, and when I am stressed I communicate that to the individual with whom I have the conflict. These are the hallmarks of secure attachment to an individual.

In practice, this means that I have a high threshold for switching from controlled to automatic mentalising – one of the features associated with secure attachment according to Luyten and Fonagy (2014) (Table 6.1). I tend to process my emotions in a fluid way, without being defensive, and I tend to trust others in my relationships. As a result, I experience curiosity about the minds of others – what Luyten and Fonagy (2014) refer to as mind-mindedness.

Part 1b — Personal Case Study

Clara & Me: Two Attachment Styles, One Relationship

"Two attachment styles. Eleven months. Continuous growth."
Leif Buechi
Secure (Type B)
  • High mentalising threshold under stress
  • Fast recovery of controlled mentalising
  • Moves toward conflict through communication
  • Trusts others, perceives little threat
  • Broaden and build cycles
Clara
Fearful-Avoidant
  • Craves closeness while fearing it
  • Approach-avoidance dynamic
  • Low threshold for activation under stress
  • Internal working model: closeness = hurt
  • Slow recovery of controlled mentalising

Clara, my girlfriend of eleven months, has a fearful-avoidant attachment style. In Bowlby's (1969) terminology, Clara simultaneously craves and fears her attachment figure — me. Thus, she feels the desire to approach the attachment figure yet also feels the desire to avoid that same attachment figure.

If Clara tends to use her avoidant defences, I will remain regulated in comparison to Clara's emotional distance. This is due to my high threshold for mentalising (Luyten and Fonagy, 2014, Table 6.1). I can hold Clara's mental state in mind and recognise that her emotional distance is not a rejection of me but rather a strategy that she utilizes for self-protection. This ability to consider another's mental state – their feelings, their desires, their wishes and their intentions – is referred to as mentalising by Luyten and Fonagy (2014).

My straightforward communicative approach serves as a corrective attachment experience, in the terms of attachment theorists, for Clara — a relational experience that corrects her expectation based on a history of trauma that intimacy always leads to injury. There are tensions. Clara can sometimes mistake my security and directness for pressure, which leads her to withdraw further. At such times, I rely on the broaden and build ability that Luyten and Fonagy (2014) assign to the securely attached: I stay interested, not reactive; I come back to the relationship, not out of need, but with a sense of curiosity.

My cultural background allowed me to remain present with Clara's complexity. The way that my father and my mother loved me provided me with two examples of love that were inherently reflective of the type of attachment described by Van Rosmalen et al. (2014).

Cross-Species Evidence

The Dog & The Tigers: Attachment Across Species

"Love knows no species — and the neuroscience agrees."

A dog raised three abandoned Bengal tiger cubs. When the tigers grew to be much larger than their mother figure, they would seek her out. This is not some viral video of a strange phenomenon. This is a phenomenon illustrating attachment theory.

This story illustrates three theoretical arguments at once. The first is about Harlow's (1958) principle of contact comfort. The tiger cubs illustrate this point as they bond with the available caretaker not the biological one. Harlow demonstrated this idea with the rhesus monkeys who preferred the mother figure made of cloth rather than the wire mother that provided milk for the infants.

Second, Solms (2021) describes the CARE circuit in the brain that activates in the dog automatically, across species lines, due to the fact that the CARE system does not discriminate against any animal but responds to infant distress signals — full stop. Third, Van Rosmalen et al. (2014) show that attachment does not have to occur through a biological tie to the attachment figure. Instead, it is the sensitive interaction between the two that creates the attachment bond.

More powerful is the suggestion of the bond between the tigers and the dog – Bowlby's (1973) internal working model suggests that the dog is the tiger's primary attachment figure during their early years and into adulthood. The findings of Van Rosmalen et al. (2014) suggest that the same relationship that forms between a child and their primary attachment figure at the age of one year old is reflected in the attachment representations that the child forms at the age of sixteen to eighteen years old.

Solms (2021) — CARE circuit across species
