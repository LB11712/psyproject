Part 1a — Theory Overview

Mark Solms & The Biology of Attachment in Mammals

"Attachment is the primal addiction — the brain's reward system is fundamentally organised around proximity to attachment figures." — Solms, M. (2021). The Hidden Spring. Profile Books.

Mark Solms, building on Jaak Panksepp's affective neuroscience, argues that attachment in mammals is not a learned behaviour — it is hardwired into subcortical emotional circuits deep in the brainstem. This biological foundation is shared across all mammalian species, from rodents to humans, and predates the evolution of the cortex entirely.

At the centre of Solms' theory is the PANIC/GRIEF circuit — the neurological engine of attachment. When a mammal is separated from its attachment figure, endorphin levels in the brain drop and the organism experiences a felt sense of acute panic. Reunion restores them. This is not metaphor — it is neurochemistry. Panksepp and Solms identified this circuit through electrical stimulation studies across mammalian species, finding identical distress vocalisations and behaviours wherever it was activated (Panksepp, 1998).

The complementary CARE circuit drives nurturing behaviour across all mammalian species. This system explains why a dog will mother tiger cubs, why a human father will sit by a hospital bed at 4am — the nurturing impulse operates below conscious choice. Unlike fish or turtles, mammals require extended caregiving after birth. This makes attachment not a luxury but an evolutionary necessity.

Solms bridges Bowlby and neuroscience in a crucial insight: what Bowlby (1969) called the "activation of the attachment system" maps directly onto these ancient subcortical circuits. The attachment behavioural system is, in essence, the PANIC/GRIEF circuit expressed in relational behaviour. As Fonagy et al. (2014) confirm, early attachment experiences forge the brain's capacity for affect regulation, attentional control, mentalisation, and self-agency — making attachment the very foundation of personality development.

Part 1b — Personal Reflection

My Attachment Style: Secure (Type B)

"Security is not the absence of difficulty — it is having someone who shows up."

I identify as securely attached — what Van Rosmalen, Van IJzendoorn and Bakermans-Kranenburg (2014) describe as a basic trust that the world is predictable and reliable, formed through sensitive and consistent caregiving. My attachment story did not emerge from a perfect household, but from a bicultural one.

I grew up with an emotionally expressive Brazilian mother and a quietly loving Swiss father — two profoundly different attachment styles, but both present when it truly mattered. As Van Rosmalen et al. (2014) note, a child can build an attachment network across multiple caregivers, each relationship unique depending on the quality of interactions. My security was built not despite this difference, but through it.

Formative Memory 1
Sailing with My Father
My father's calm, steady presence on the water regulated my nervous system without a single word. This is Bowlby's (1969) secure base made visceral — felt safety through proximity, the body knowing it is protected.
Formative Memory 2
4am at the Hospital
My mother rushed me to hospital during a pericarditis episode, my systems spiralling. Her immediate, unconditional response was Solms' (2021) CARE circuit in action — the mammalian nurturing drive that operates below conscious choice.

My father's later transformation in Hawaii — arriving burdened with emotional luggage and leaving it behind forever — illustrates what Van Rosmalen et al. (2014) call the internal working model: a mental representation of attachment that continually adjusts to new experience. His shift made him more emotionally present, deepening my secure base.

Fonagy et al. (2014) confirm that securely attached individuals are independently rated as more resilient, self-reliant, socially oriented and empathic, with deeper relationships and higher self-esteem. In practice: I handle conflict directly through open communication, I remain calm under stress, and when stressed I communicate it rather than suppress or amplify it. These are the hallmarks of secure attachment.

In practice, my secure attachment manifests as a high threshold for switching from controlled to automatic mentalising under stress — what Luyten and Fonagy (2014) describe as one of the defining features of secure attachment (Table 6.1). I process emotions in a fluid and non-defensive way, trusting others and perceiving little environmental threat. This enables genuine curiosity about the inner worlds of those I am close to — what Luyten and Fonagy (2014) call mind-mindedness.

Part 1b — Personal Case Study

Clara & Me: Two Attachment Styles, One Relationship

"Two attachment styles. Eleven months. Continuous growth."
Leif Buechi
Secure (Type B)
  • High mentalising threshold under stress
  • Fast recovery of controlled mentalising
  • Moves toward conflict through communication
  • Trusts others, perceives little threat
  • Broaden and build cycles
Clara
Fearful-Avoidant
  • Craves closeness while fearing it
  • Approach-avoidance dynamic
  • Low threshold for activation under stress
  • Internal working model: closeness = hurt
  • Slow recovery of controlled mentalising

Clara, my girlfriend of eleven months, has a fearful-avoidant attachment style — simultaneously craving closeness and fearing it. In Bowlby's (1969) terms, her internal working model carries both the longing for a secure base and the expectation that it will be withdrawn. This produces an approach-avoidance dynamic in which the attachment figure — me — is both desired and threatening.

When Clara activates her avoidant defences — pulling back, needing space, becoming emotionally distant — my secure attachment means I remain regulated rather than pursuing frantically or withdrawing in kind. This is my high mentalising threshold in action (Luyten and Fonagy, 2014, Table 6.1). I am able to hold Clara's mental state in mind: understanding her withdrawal as a self-protective strategy rather than rejection — the essence of what Luyten and Fonagy (2014) define as mentalising: the capacity to envision another in terms of their feelings, desires, wishes and intentions.

My direct communication style functions as what attachment theorists call a corrective attachment experience — a relational encounter that gently challenges Clara's fearful expectation that closeness inevitably leads to hurt. Tensions do exist. Clara can occasionally misread my security and directness as pressure, triggering further withdrawal. In these moments, I draw on the broaden and build capacity that Luyten and Fonagy (2014) associate with secure attachment — staying curious rather than reactive, returning to the relationship with openness rather than demand.

My bicultural upbringing equipped me to remain present with Clara's complexity. My father's quiet steadiness and my mother's fierce, unconditional responsiveness gave me two complementary models of love — models that together mirror the very attachment network described by Van Rosmalen et al. (2014).

Cross-Species Evidence

The Dog & The Tigers: Attachment Across Species

"Love knows no species — and the neuroscience agrees."

A dog raised three abandoned Bengal tiger cubs. Now fully grown — vastly larger than their surrogate mother — the tigers still seek her out, still recognise her as their primary attachment figure. This is not a viral curiosity. It is attachment theory made visible.

This story illustrates three core theoretical arguments simultaneously. First, Harlow's (1958) contact comfort principle: attachment forms with the available caregiver, not the biological one. The tiger cubs bonded to warmth, presence and responsiveness — not genetics. Harlow demonstrated the same principle with rhesus monkeys who chose a cloth surrogate mother over a wire one that provided milk.

Second, Solms' (2021) CARE circuit: the nurturing drive activated in the dog automatically, across species lines, because the CARE system does not discriminate. It responds to infant distress signals — full stop. Third, Van Rosmalen et al. (2014) confirm that attachment does not depend on a biological tie with the attachment figure. Sensitive interaction is what creates the bond.

Most powerfully, the tigers' enduring bond mirrors Bowlby's (1973) internal working model: the dog is encoded as their primary attachment figure in early life and remains so into adulthood. This maps precisely onto what Van Rosmalen et al. (2014) describe — a secure attachment relationship at age one predicts secure attachment representations sixteen to eighteen years later.

Solms (2021) — CARE circuit across species
